# Abstract

This study investigates the effects of spaced retrieval practice on long-term retention
of declarative knowledge in undergraduate students. Using a randomised controlled design,
we compared three study conditions over a six-week period. Results indicate that spaced
retrieval practice significantly outperforms massed practice and passive re-reading across
all retention intervals. Effect sizes were large and consistent across subgroups.

# 1. Introduction

Memory consolidation remains a central challenge in applied educational research.
Despite decades of evidence supporting the testing effect and spacing effect, most
students default to massed study strategies that produce inferior long-term retention.

## 1.1 Background

The spacing effect — the finding that distributing practice over time leads to better
retention than massing practice in a single session — was first documented by Ebbinghaus
in 1885. Subsequent laboratory studies consistently replicated the finding across verbal,
procedural, and conceptual material. Cepeda et al. (2006) conducted a meta-analysis of
254 studies and found a median effect size of d = 0.46 favouring spaced over massed
practice at test delays of one week or longer.

Retrieval practice — actively recalling information rather than passively restudying it —
produces an independent and complementary benefit. The retrieval practice effect is robust
across content domains, age groups, and educational levels. Combined with spacing,
retrieval practice produces retention advantages that are not explained by additional
study time alone.

## 1.2 Research Questions

The present study addressed three research questions:

1. Does spaced retrieval practice produce superior retention compared to massed
   retrieval practice at a one-week delayed test?
2. Does the advantage of spaced retrieval practice persist at a six-week delayed test?
3. Are the retention benefits moderated by individual differences in working memory
   capacity or prior knowledge?

# 2. Methods

## 2.1 Participants

A total of 180 undergraduate students were recruited from an introductory psychology
course at a mid-sized research university. Participants received partial course credit
for participation. Following exclusions for missing data (n = 12) and non-compliance
with the study schedule (n = 8), the final sample comprised 160 participants.

## 2.2 Procedure

Participants were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: spaced retrieval,
massed retrieval, or passive re-reading (control). The study proceeded as follows:

1. Participants attended a 90-minute initial encoding session in which they read a
   3,200-word passage on the cellular mechanisms of memory consolidation.
2. Participants in the spaced retrieval condition completed five short-answer retrieval
   practice sessions distributed across the following two weeks (Days 2, 4, 7, 10, 14).
3. Participants in the massed retrieval condition completed all five retrieval practice
   sessions within a single two-hour session on Day 2.
4. Participants in the control condition re-read the passage on Day 2 for the same
   total time as the massed retrieval group.
5. All participants completed a criterion test at Day 7 (one-week delay) and again at
   Day 42 (six-week delay). Tests were identical in format and drawn from the same
   item pool.
6. Participants completed the working memory capacity measure during their first session.

## 2.3 Data Analysis

Retention scores were analysed using a 3 (Condition) x 2 (Test delay) mixed ANOVA with
condition as a between-subjects factor and test delay as a within-subjects factor. Effect
sizes are reported as partial eta-squared. Post-hoc comparisons used Tukey's HSD procedure.
Moderation analyses used hierarchical multiple regression with working memory capacity
and prior knowledge as continuous predictors.

# 3. Results

## 3.1 Primary Outcomes

The omnibus ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of condition,
F(2, 157) = 34.81, p < 0.001, partial η² = 0.31. There was also a significant
Condition x Test Delay interaction, F(2, 157) = 8.24, p < 0.001, partial η² = 0.10,
indicating that the retention advantage of spaced practice grew larger at the six-week
compared to the one-week delay.

Post-hoc comparisons indicated that all three conditions differed significantly from
each other at both time points (all p < 0.01). The spaced retrieval group showed the
smallest decline from one-week to six-week testing (6.9 percentage points), compared
to 14.5 points for massed retrieval and 13.4 points for the control group.

## 3.2 Subgroup Analysis

Working memory capacity moderated the effect of condition at the six-week delay
(interaction β = 0.18, p = 0.03), such that participants with higher WMC showed a
larger benefit from spaced retrieval. Prior knowledge did not significantly moderate
the condition effect at either delay (both p > 0.15).

# 4. Discussion

## 4.1 Interpretation

The results provide strong support for the efficacy of spaced retrieval practice in
naturalistic educational settings. The large effect sizes observed (d = 1.42 at six
weeks for spaced vs. control) are consistent with laboratory research and exceed typical
estimates from meta-analytic summaries, possibly reflecting the use of meaningful academic
content rather than laboratory word lists.

The growing advantage of spaced practice at the six-week delay is theoretically important.
It suggests that the benefits of spaced retrieval are not merely due to increased total
study time — the massed retrieval group had equivalent practice time — but reflect a
qualitative difference in how memories are consolidated and indexed. This is consistent
with the desirable difficulties framework proposed by Bjork (1994), which argues that
conditions that introduce manageable retrieval challenges during encoding produce more
durable and transferable memory representations.

The moderation by working memory capacity is a novel finding. One interpretation is that
participants with higher WMC are better positioned to benefit from retrieval practice
because they can engage in more elaborative processing during retrieval attempts. Future
research should examine whether providing scaffolding during retrieval (e.g., partial cues)
attenuates the WMC moderation.

## 4.2 Limitations

This study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results:

- The participant sample was drawn from a single university introductory psychology pool,
  limiting generalisability to other populations and educational contexts.
- The passage used for encoding was limited to one content domain (cellular memory
  mechanisms). Results may differ for procedural knowledge, mathematics, or skills-based
  learning.
- Compliance with the spaced study schedule was monitored via completion timestamps, but
  participants in the massed condition could have engaged in additional informal studying
  that was not captured.
- The study did not include a transfer test; it is unclear whether the retention
  advantages generalise to novel questions or applied problem-solving tasks.
- The working memory capacity measure (automated operation span) assesses one component
  of WMC; results may differ with other WMC assessments.

# 5. Conclusion

Spaced retrieval practice produced substantially superior long-term retention compared to
both massed retrieval practice and passive re-reading, with advantages growing larger at
a six-week compared to a one-week delay. The benefits were moderated by working memory
capacity, suggesting that future interventions may benefit from adapting spacing schedules
to individual learner characteristics. These findings support the implementation of
spaced retrieval tools in formal educational settings.
